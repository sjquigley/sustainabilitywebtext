Exploring Sustainable Design

ABSTRACT

This case study examines how we might advance inclusive models of science communication working with youth at an environmental science and code education day camp focused on exploring sustainable design. Over the course of a single day, our middle school participants explore and inquire into sustainable design sites, gather data, and work with HTML code templates to communicate their findings to others. This case study examines how and why we might leverage inclusive communication models, digital tools, and the available means of persuasion to center youth in the communication of sustainable design. Our case study introduces participants to a sustainable design dialogic communication framework we use to promote inquiry and dialogue. We similarly use this framework to help students extends these conversations through code-based multimodal making methods.

INTRODUCTION

This case study examines how we might advance inclusive models of science communication working with youth at an environmental science and code education day camp focused on exploring sustainable design. Over the course of a single day, our middle school participants explore and inquire into sustainable design sites, gather data, and work with HTML code templates to communicate their findings to others. This case study examines how and why we might leverage inclusive communication models, digital tools, and the available means of persuasion to center youth in the communication of sustainable design.

What is sustainable design? That’s the question we want our youth participants to spend day working through. The architect Jason McLennan (2004) defines sustainable design as “a design philosophy that seeks to maximize the quality of the built environment, while minimizing or eliminating negative impact to the natural environment” (p 4). But even McLennan is quick to point out the problem of defining as well of the misuse of sustainability related terms. We want our participants to understand sustainable design as a practice, one that requires another set of questions and sub-questions to language, enact, interpret, and maintain. These questions, what we refer to as the Sustainable Design Dialogic Communication Framework, are geared towards unconcealing the context of a specific sustainable design act. These questions include but are not limited to: What is it and what does it do? How does it work? What is the impact? and What should we do next? or How can we do it better?)

The practice of sustainable design requires a synthesis of multiple knowledges and methods best interpreted through the study of sustainability science, an area of research that examines the relationship between humans and nature and develops sustainable solutions to address imbalance (Robert Kates et al., 2001). Because the field emerged from conversations around sustainable development, it seeks to operationalize knowledge, however, early contributors to the field disagreed on strategy. Some, David Cash et al. (2003) for example, imagined science and technology experts as thought leaders: facilitating relationships with decision makers, translating scientific knowledge, and mediating disputes with those who might interfere with policy action. Others like William Clark & Nancy Dickinson. (2003), advocated for a bottom-up approach, using place-based methods informed by local perspectives and knowledges. Still others, including Anthony McMichael et al. (2003), understood environmental problems as largely caused by human factors stemming from resource imbalances, poor market choices, and ignorance, and thus called on a fields outside of the environmental sciences, including geography, economics, and epidemiology, to prioritize environmental issues in their discourse and collaborate across disciplines to work towards sustainable solutions.

Today, the field of sustainability science requires a wide range of natural scientists, social scientists, and even humanities scholars, working collaboratively to solve problems related to environmental sustainability (Anne Jerneck et al., 2011). Sustainability science adopts a social-ecological systems approach to understanding human and nature interaction as networked and occurring within and across local, regional, and global scales (Joern Fischer et al., 2015). A social-ecological systems approach also interprets environmental sustainability solutions as inextricably linked to issues of equity and social justice (Melissa Leach et al., 2010). Similarly, in Braiding Sweet Grass (2013), Robin Kimmerer shares the indigenous concept of reciprocity, the equitable and just giving and taking of only what one needs and shares how this concept is enacted both in nature and in indigenous cultures. And while she provides a surplus of science evidence explaining the benefits of reciprocity, she cautions us that at this moment in time, “it is not more data that we need […] but more wisdom” (p. 415). It is the task of sustainability science to recognize such wisdom, and to synthesize different knowledge systems, especially indigenous knowledge, and translate this knowledge into mutually agreed upon sustainability best practices and policy (Maria Tengö et al., 2017). In addition to aligning research and knowledge with policy an practice, institutions of higher education can advance sustainability goals grant funding, training for the industries and communities they serve, and other modes of outreach (Issa Berchin et al. 2021).

Carpenter, Serena et al. (2016).

Inclusive models of science communication factor prominently in achieving the goals of sustainability science (Laura Lindfield et al., 2012). This case study explores the concept of a “tour” as a method for engaging our youth participants in dialogue. Ellen Trahan et al. (2017) discuss the role of sustainability tours in raising the profile of sustainability initiatives on campuses; however, while these authors focus on lecture-based tours, we favor a learner-led approach more in line with Spring Gillard and Rob VanWynsberghe (2021). To structure our learner led approach, we implement the Sustainable Design Dialogic Communication Framework (SDDCF), a version of stasis theory designed to promote inquiry and structure dialogue, and later to help our participants conversations through code-based multimodal making methods. While these tools and methods could provide meaningful learning experiences for learners of any age, this outreach centers youth and seeks to provide meaningful opportunities for youth to form knowledge, agency and identities around issues of environmental sustainability.

YOUTH AND THE COMMUNICATION OF SCIENCE

Since the first volume of Le Journal des Sçavans on January 5th, 1665, the diffusion of scientific knowledge has largely followed the same linear progression. It begins with the scientists, those experts who hypothesize, gather data, form knowledge, and publish their findings. Next, this knowledge is abstracted and disseminated to the public by mediators and powerbrokers, i.e., journalist, politicians, educators, business entities, even movie stars. Science communicators have long referred to this linear progression as the deficit model of science communication, in that it assumes scientist have the tools, bone fides, and authority the public lack. But over the last thirty years, the field of science communication, aided by social epistemic theory and ethnographic methods, has come to understand the public’s scientific knowledge and interests in greater complexity (Terry Burns et al., 2003), as more “active, knowledgeable, playing multiple roles, receiving as well as shaping science” (Edna Einsiedel, 2007, p.5). This is not to say that in the years since, the deficit model has been wholly upended (Brian Trench, 2008) or that scientist play a diminished role in the formation of scientific knowledge (Carina Cortassa, 2016). Rather, what we can claim, is that our understanding of what constitutes science communication has broadened. For example, Brian Trench and Massimiano Bucchi (2021) theorize science communication along a continuum of modes, models, and applications ranging from exclusive to inclusive: on one end, they locate the aforementioned deficit model and other top-down approaches, and on the other, bottom-up participatory models encouraging the public to take part in both the formation and communication of scientific knowledge through activities like citizen science. Between these polls, they place dialogic models meant to engage the public in inquiry, discussion, and deliberation. To further their discussion, the authors challenge science communicators to examine the injustice and inequity inherent in these models, but also their efficacy “in terms of how, and how much, a given practice or set of practices stimulate wider conversation” (p.9) For strategic, ethical, and legal reasons, we should also consider where do our youth fit on such a continuum?

There are many reasons why we should prioritize youth in science communication. We should start by recognizing that children have a powerful ability to influence one another in areas of science participation (Glynis Breakwell & Sue Beardsell, 1992) and environmental activism (Hannah Wallis and Laura S. Loy, 2021). Children also have the ability to influence adults, as demonstrated by the “Greta Thunberg Effect” (Anandita Sabherwal et al., 2021); the eponymous phenomenon explaining how individuals of various ages are more likely to participate in collective action against climate change after having heard Thunberg’s message. Science communicators have also recognized youths’ ability to leverage social media, especially around issues related to environmental justice. Francesca Belotti et al. (2022) document how youth climate activist master a range of social media tools and tailor messages for specific users in order to build networks out of disparate nodes to achieve a variety of organizational, educational, and political goals related to climate action. Recognizing this skill, other scholars believe scientists should work more directly with youth to create social media messaging, build consensus, and deliver policy targets (Eide, Elisabeth & Risto Kunelius. 2021). Science communication strategy is especially important in the area of environmental sciences, where attitudes about the environment range wildly and the communication medium plays an important role in whether or not individuals choose to listen (Jessica Bolin & Lawrence Hamilton 2018).

Taking a different line argument, Matteo Mergoza and Tricia Jenkins (2013) see the inclusion of children in the communication of science as an ethical imperative because children will pay the greatest price for or our current science policies and practices. They challenge science communicators to develop new dialogic models of science communication that center youth and their own exigencies as they relate to environmental science and policy. “Through such an approach, science in the perception of young people can indeed become a tool to design a world as close as possible to the world in which they would like to live, rather than external knowledge that a child just needs to learn in order to live in whatever world is proposed to him or her” (p.3). And still other science communicators emphasize the legal reasons for doing so; Elizabeth Welty and Laura Lundy’s (2013) “voice model for involving youth in decision making” cites the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (1989) which codified a child’s right to “scientific and technical knowledge” (Article 28) and the right to form and “express […] views freely in all matters affecting the child, the views of the child being given due weight in accordance with the age and maturity of the child” (Article 12) and that “[t]he child shall have the right to freedom of expression; this right shall include freedom to seek, receive and impart information and ideas of all kinds, regardless of frontiers, either orally, in writing or in print, in the form of art, or through any other media of the child's choice” (Article 13). Working from these first principles, Welty and Lundy advocate for the empowerment of our youth as active participants in shaping policies and practices that affect their lives and underscore the pivotal role of science education and communication methods play in this process. Every child, they argue, has a right to:

  1. Space – safe and inclusive opportunity to form and express a view
  2. Voice – facilitated to express views freely in medium of choice
  3. Audience – the view must be listened to
  4. Influence – the view must be acted upon

–Welty & Lundy, 2013

Following Welty and Lundy’s voice model, this research adopts a hybrid approach to creating an inclusive science communication experience for our youth that includes spaces for dialogic inquiry, tools for voicing this knowledge, and opportunities for children to circulate their findings to an audience, and thus the opportunity to influence policy.

DIALOGIC COMMUNICATION TOOLS

A sociocultural approach to knowledge formation emphasizes the dynamic interaction between individuals and their cultural and social contexts in the construction and transmission of knowledge. Rooted in the early twentieth century work of Lev Vygotsky (1978) and his theory of sociocultural development, this approach views knowledge as co-constructed through social interactions within specific cultural settings. It recognizes that individuals are not passive recipients of knowledge, but active participants in its creation and dissemination. Per Linell (2009) notes that active participation, what we refer to as dialogic communication, relates to how human beings make sense of the world through “meaning making activities that are mediated in and through language, words, signs, symbols, or concepts’’ (p.4). Dialogic science communication seeks to bridge the gap between experts and the public through both the communication and formation of scientific knowledge. However, the dialogic model of science communication does not occur in a specific moment in space and time. Rather, we should, as researchers and interlocutors, understand it as inherently complex, yet fecund. As Bridie McGreavy et al. argue (2015), “Approaching communication as a complex system means that our interventions are always incomplete because human interactions are recursively guided by context-specific structures and processes that are always in a state of becoming” (p.9). The complexity of human dialogue requires iteration; interlocutors must engage with and consider the knowledge and perspectives of others and reassert or reorganize their thinking based on a given exchange. Dialogic communication, as an active communication strategy, also requires a great deal of effort and accountability from all parties. Participants and organizations engaged in dialogue must yield control while opening themselves to uncertain outcomes and other risks (Kent, Michael L., & Taylor, Maureen, (2002). Dialogic communication models have proven their value in the context of working with youth: Kristen Marcell et al. (2004) have demonstrated that youth are more likely to internalize environmental knowledge through dialogic models. Other researchers have demonstrated the benefits of using such models in diverse settings working with populations of vastly different backgrounds (Rupert Wegerif et al, 2013).

Dialogic science communication theory and methods naturally overlap with research in the field of education, which focuses more narrowly on how learners actively engage with both educators and peers through meaningful dialogue. Some of this research focuses both on teacher-centered learning spaces where science instructors use inquiry to bridge cognitive divides (Mortimer, Eduardo & Scott, Phillip, 2003), decenter authority (Scott, Phillip, & Ametller, Juame, 2007), and assess student knowledge (Neil Mercer et al., 2009), while others favor the dialogic as means of empowering students, giving each the tools to initiate and facilitate meaningful dialogue on their own. Rosalind Driver et al. (2000), for example present the case for teaching argument to promote scientific norms in the secondary science classroom. Argument, they say, mirrors how scientists, themselves, construct knowledge. While these researchers briefly discuss argument as “debate,” they define the term more broadly, explaining argument as an individual and collective act, taking many forms including written and spoken discourse. They say that in addition to helping students understand the social epistemic nature of science, argument prepares students for civic engagement. Although they feature Stephen Toulmin’s (1958: 2003) work on argumentation as a framework for understanding how argument occurs in natural language, they caution that understanding argumentative structure does not necessarily assist students in assessing the value of the actual scientific data. Here, they seem to be saying that teaching argumentation also requires educators to teach the kinds of critical thinking skills needed to analyze, assess and communicate data. Other researchers see the value in prescribing Toulmin more strategically, as “patterns” students can use to 1. analyze and structure scientific arguments and 2. engage the ideas of others through “dialogical argumentation” (Sibel Erduran, et al, 2004). In the first case, the researchers promoted Toulmin argument patterns (TAP) to help students structure conversations around science topics. In the second, they required students use TAP while working in small groups more akin to debate. In this latter case, students were asked to acknowledge and incorporate their interlocutor’s argument within their own by way rebuttal. The work of Stan Frijters et al (2008) demonstrate that debate strategies requiring students to take a specific position on an issue are highly effective in building critical thinking skills and science knowledge in youth. However, they also warn of its overuse, in that debate can require a great deal of energy on the part of students. Their research found that the more students engaged in debate, the less they liked this mode of instruction.

This sustainable design case study is similarly interested in the using argument as a tool to structure dialogic engagement, but less interested in defining argument as adversarial or debate oriented. Because of the exploratory, co-creative nature of our project, we see the value of using stasis theory as a tool for both guiding inquiry and structuring argument. A range of ancient rhetoricians contributed to stasis theory and its application from Aristotle and Hermagoras in Ancient Greece to Cicero and Quintilian in Ancient Rome (Sharon Crowley and Debra Hawhee, 1999). Modern rhetoricians have similarly addressed the use of stasis theory in science (Jeanne Fahnestock and Maria Secor, 1988) and engineering (Suzanne Lane, 2022) contexts. This latter research suggests stasis theory can equip engineering students with a “framework of critical analysis [that] can help to turn a host of conflicting claims and multiple uncertainties into an ordered process of problem solving” (p.3). Greek stasis theory involves four questions, that of: conjecture (What happened?), definition (What is it?), quality (What are the implications?), and policy (What should we do next?). Janet Davis (1996) argues that stasis theory provides a valuable tool for inquiring into a topic (judicial rhetoric), making judgements (epideictic rhetoric), and forming policy (deliberative rhetoric). This case study similarly explores how we might adapt and apply stasis theory as a dialogic communication tool for promoting inquiry in the field and constructing knowledge in deliberative workshop spaces.

Data collection and analysis figure prominently in the three questions of stasis theory, that of conjecture, definition, and quality. These first three questions also align with Dewey’s (1933:2022) understanding of human beings as innately curious for knowledge and thus predisposed to do the work of science. Dewey, and the empiricist who followed him, believe that individuals learn best observing, testing, and reasoning through direct engagement with scientific phenomena (68-72). In that humans are innately curious, “All knowledge, all science, thus aims to grasp the meaning of objects and events” (p. 117). It is no wonder that later constructivist methods especially their focus on inquiry and problem-solving have their origins in Dewey’s work (Johnston, James Scott, 2013). Crowd sourcing apps like “Cornell Bird Lab,” “iNaturalist,” and “Seek” try to harness this same curiosity, inviting the user to investigate nature and even collaborate in the data collection process. In the case of iNaturalist, the app follows best practices (Ulrike Sturm et al, 2018) by affording location data sharing, media file uploads, and forums to share “observations” in significant detail. While many researchers see digital media as a tool for advancing citizen science, others note that not all crowd sourcing apps contribute significantly to the formation of environmental knowledge. Tools that afford users the ability to analyze their data, especially in relation to other user data will do the most to allow individuals to make judgements and form knowledge (Joseph Roche et al, 2020). This case study is also interested in the fourth question of stasis, that of policy, helping our participants think about what should we do next?. While we see our participants understanding of policy emerging in the field, we imagine it will solidify in the workshop space where students work alongside one another building their science communication document.

DATA COLLECTION AND DELIBERATIVE MAKING

Data collection, analysis, and judgement figure prominently in the first three questions of stasis theory: conjecture: (Where does this occur?), definition (What is it?), and quality (Why is it important?). They also align with Dewey’s understanding of human beings as innately curious for knowledge and thus predisposed to do the work of science. Dewey, and the empiricist who followed him, believe that due to human curiosity, individuals learned best through direct engagement with scientific phenomena by deductively or inductively observing, testing, and reasoning (68-72) and that without such lines of inquiry, life would be nonsensical. “All knowledge, all science, thus aims to grasp the meaning of objects and events” Dewey 1933; 2022 p.117). It’s no wonder that constructivist related methods including inquiry and problem-solving have their origins in Dewey’s work (Johnston, J. S. (2013). Similarly, many crowd sourcing apps like “Cornell Bird Lab,” “iNaturalist,” and “Seek” invite the user to investigate nature and even collaborate in the data collection process. In the case of iNaturalist, the app offers opportunities for the user to share location data, media files, and record “observations” in significant detail. While some researchers see digital media as a tool for advancing citizen science, others note that not all crowd sourcing apps contribute significantly to the formation of knowledge. For example, Roche et al (2020) argue that those who design citizen science tools must prioritize environmental knowledge along with data collection as user experience goals. In addition to collecting data, apps that afford users the ability to analyze their data especially in relation to other user data will do the most to allow individuals to make judgements and form knowledge.

It is also important to think about how explicitly these tools address questions of deliberation. If these apps do not afford opportunities for communicative engagement with other users, we should consider how use of these apps correlates with engagement elsewhere, in virtual and actual spaces. In addition to data collection and analysis, this project is interested in creating a deliberative workshop space and empowering students with tools to make their voices heard. For Sennet (2012), a deliberative workshop space allows for divergent thinking processes so that each might learn from the knowledges and skills of others. Rather than seeing craft as stagnant, Sennet imagines a creative synergy emerging in a shared workshop space, but also dialogic communicative practice, where different theories and methods are practiced, discussed, and judged alongside one another. A deliberative workshop space fosters creativity, knowledge, and practice that would not be possible if we worked independent of one another. Like Bussey (2023), we hope to use such a workshop space to develop a “communicative praxis […] as a platform for joint construction of meaning around sustainability practices as well as for co-creation of knowledge and practices that could enhance our capability to act and navigate wicked sustainability issues” (p.50).
